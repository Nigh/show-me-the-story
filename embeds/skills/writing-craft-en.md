# Core Writing Craft (English)

This skill follows mainstream English-fiction craft conventions (Sanderson's promise/progress/payoff, Swain's scene-and-sequel, the Save-the-Cat scene framework). It is not a translation of any Chinese-web-fiction framework; the rhythms and reader expectations of English trade and serial fiction are different.

## 7 chapter-opening hooks

Pick one. Never open two adjacent chapters with the same kind.

1. **In medias res action** — open mid-action, then briefly back-fill the why.
2. **Sharp question** — a line of dialogue or interior thought that is a question the reader cannot yet answer.
3. **Inversion of expectation** — set up a routine in one sentence, break it in the next.
4. **Sensory anchor** — a smell, sound, weight, or temperature so specific it places the reader instantly. Not the sky. Not the weather.
5. **Cold dialogue** — open on a line that only makes sense once the scene catches up.
6. **Time skip + change** — "Three weeks later, the door was different."
7. **Direct stakes** — name what is on the line in this chapter within the first paragraph.

Forbidden openings (LLM defaults that read as slop): a sky / weather paragraph; "In a world where…"; a character waking up; a mirror description; a "little did they know" pre-narration.

## 13 chapter-ending hooks

Cycle through; never end two adjacent chapters on the same kind.

1. New character arrives with implied stakes.
2. Old character returns under new circumstances.
3. Object / letter / message / call arrives carrying information the reader can partially read.
4. A decision must be made before morning.
5. Two threads converge that the reader hadn't connected.
6. A physical change to the body or environment that hints at the next chapter's problem.
7. The plan just agreed on cannot work — and the POV character knows it.
8. A line of dialogue that reframes everything that came before.
9. The clock starts (deadline, countdown, opponent moves).
10. The POV character sees something the reader does not — yet.
11. Quiet beat after a loss that reframes the next chapter's emotional question.
12. A small kindness from an unexpected source.
13. A specific physical action interrupted by an offscreen sound.

Forbidden closers: a symbolic landscape image about light/silence/distance/destiny; "to be continued"; "and so it was that…"; a moralising summary; a foreshadowing aside in the narrator's voice ("Little did she know…").

## Promise / progress / payoff (Sanderson framework)

Every chapter must do one or more of:

- **Open a promise**: introduce a question, threat, or relational tension the reader now expects to be resolved.
- **Progress an open promise**: increase tension, narrow options, reveal new information, change power.
- **Pay off an open promise**: deliver a resolution, a turn, an answered question, or a meaningful loss.

Audit at chapter level:

- Track every open promise as it appears. Retire at least one promise every 3 chapters; introduce at most two per chapter.
- A chapter that opens but neither progresses nor pays off any existing promise reads as filler.
- A chapter with three or more new promises and no payoff reads as a stall.

## Scene and sequel pacing (Swain)

Within a chapter:

- **Scene**: goal → conflict → setback (or partial win). Drives forward.
- **Sequel**: reaction → dilemma → decision. Lets the reader breathe and gives the character a chance to choose the next goal.

Default rhythm: scene → short sequel → scene → short sequel. Skip the sequel only deliberately, never accidentally. Two scenes in a row without a sequel becomes exhausting; two sequels in a row stalls the book.

## Dialogue economy

- Cut greetings ("Hello", "How are you") unless they carry subtext or stakes.
- Cut acknowledgements ("yes", "okay", "I see") unless silence would be wrong.
- Argument scenes: characters interrupt and overlap. Use sentence fragments, em-dash cut-offs (sparingly), and unanswered questions.
- Tag economy: "said" is invisible. Avoid said-bookisms ("exclaimed", "expostulated", "queried"). Replace adverbial tags ("she said sadly") with action beats inside the line ("She set the cup down. 'I know.'").
- No more than 3 consecutive lines of dialogue without a body beat, a sensory anchor, or an interior reaction in close POV.

## Character voice

Each major character has, established within their first two scenes:

- One **verbal tic** (a recurring phrase, hedge, filler, or curse).
- One **syntactic preference** (short clipped sentences vs. winding sentences with embedded clauses).
- One **topic they steer toward** under stress.
- One **topic they refuse**.

Voice is not accent. Carry voice in word choice, rhythm, and silence. If you swap two characters' dialogue tags and the lines still read correctly, the voices are not differentiated enough — rewrite one.

## Sensory grounding

Every scene anchors at least **two senses other than sight** in its first half-page. Trade-fiction readers expect smell, sound, texture, weight, or temperature — not paragraph after paragraph of visual description.

## Pacing control by scene type

| Scene type | Sentence length | Paragraph length | Dialogue density | Sensory mode |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Action / fight | very short, fragments allowed | 1–3 sentences | sparse, clipped | kinetic, body |
| Tension / suspense | mixed, with occasional long winding sentences holding off resolution | varied | mid | sound, smell, temperature |
| Dialogue-heavy | mid to long for inner voice, short for exchanges | speaker change = paragraph break | dense | minimal — let dialogue carry |
| Reflection / sequel | longer, more complex | 4–7 sentences | rare | one anchoring sense, often touch or smell |
| Description / setting | mid | 2–4 sentences before character reaction | none | rotate senses, never pure visual |

## Continuity rules

- **One-time events** (first meetings, identity reveals, deaths, declarations, kisses) happen exactly once and are *referenced* — never *re-enacted* — afterwards.
- **Established physical detail** (a scar, a missing button, a borrowed coat, a limp) should recur naturally across the next three chapters before fading.
- **Setup / payoff distance**: a planted detail pays off best 3–8 chapters later. Pay-offs within the same chapter feel obvious; pay-offs more than 15 chapters later feel forgotten.

## Per-chapter shape (default; adapt freely)

```
Opening   (~10%)   hook + bridge from previous chapter's final beat
Rising    (~60%)   2–3 scene/sequel cycles that progress at least one promise
Turn      (~20%)   the chapter's defining beat (revelation, decision, reversal, loss)
Close     (~10%)   ending hook + clean exit beat
```

Length is governed by function, not by quota. A payoff chapter can be short; a turning-point chapter usually wants room. Resist the LLM tendency to make every chapter the same length.
